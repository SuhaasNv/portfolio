Concept Case Study

EasyMeals

A subscription-based healthy meal delivery service that helps busy professionals maintain nutrition without the time investment of meal planning and cooking.

Context & Problem

This case study was developed as part of the Designing and Managing Products and Platforms course at NUS on January 15, 2026, led by faculty member Scott Si. The course focused on applying design thinking methodologies to real-world product challenges. This exercise emphasized structured problem-solving, user-centered design, and business reasoning—core skills for product management.

Problem Statement

Busy working professionals struggle to maintain healthy eating habits due to time constraints. The gap between wanting to eat well and having the time to plan, shop, and prepare nutritious meals creates a persistent pain point that affects both physical health and daily productivity.

Why This Problem Matters

Poor nutrition among working professionals has measurable impacts: decreased energy levels, reduced productivity, and long-term health risks. Existing solutions (meal prep services, restaurant delivery, grocery delivery) either require significant time investment or compromise on nutritional quality. There's a clear opportunity for a solution that removes the time barrier while maintaining nutritional standards.

User Research & Insights

Research Planning

Before conducting research, we developed a structured research plan to ensure we gathered meaningful insights. The plan outlined our research objectives, target user segments, interview questions, and methods for synthesizing findings. This upfront planning helped us stay focused on understanding the core problem rather than getting lost in surface-level symptoms.

How Insights Were Gathered

Through structured interviews and empathy mapping exercises, we engaged with working professionals across different industries. The research focused on understanding daily routines, pain points around meal planning, and existing coping strategies.

Key Pain Points

  • Time scarcity: Planning meals, grocery shopping, and cooking compete with work demands and personal time
  • Decision fatigue: Constant choices about what to eat drain mental energy
  • Nutritional knowledge gap: Uncertainty about what constitutes a balanced meal for their lifestyle
  • Inconsistent habits: Good intentions don't translate to consistent behavior due to lack of structure

Core Insight

The real barrier isn't cost or preference—it's cognitive load. Professionals want healthy meals but need a system that removes the mental overhead of planning and decision-making. They're willing to pay for convenience if it delivers consistent, high-quality nutrition without requiring their time or attention.

Problem Reframing (HMW)

Framing the Problem

Before developing solutions, we spent time reframing the problem from multiple angles. This process involved examining the problem from different perspectives: the user's perspective, the business perspective, and the system perspective. By framing the problem correctly, we ensured that our solutions would address root causes rather than symptoms.

Final How Might We Statement

How might we help busy professionals maintain consistent, healthy eating habits without requiring time for meal planning or cooking?

Why This Framing Was Chosen

This HMW statement focuses on the core constraint (time) while leaving room for multiple solution approaches. It emphasizes outcomes (consistent, healthy eating) rather than prescribing a specific solution type. The framing also acknowledges that the target users are willing to invest resources (money) but not time—a critical distinction for business model design.

Ideation & Exploration

We generated multiple solution concepts through structured brainstorming sessions, exploring different approaches to addressing the core problem:

  • Meal kit delivery: Pre-portioned ingredients with recipes (deprioritized due to still requiring cooking time)
  • AI meal planner: Personalized meal plans with automated grocery lists (deprioritized due to complexity and still requiring shopping/cooking)
  • Restaurant partnership platform: Curated healthy options from local restaurants (deprioritized due to quality control challenges and higher costs)
  • Ready-to-eat meal delivery: Fully prepared, nutritionally balanced meals delivered on schedule (selected as primary solution)
  • In-app fitness integration: Connect meal recommendations with fitness goals (selected as supporting feature)

Why Some Ideas Were Deprioritized

Ideas that still required user time (cooking, shopping) were eliminated because they didn't address the core constraint. Solutions with high operational complexity or quality control challenges were deprioritized due to feasibility concerns. The focus remained on solutions that could deliver consistent value with minimal user effort.

Prioritization & Decision-Making

Impact vs Feasibility Analysis

We evaluated each solution concept using an impact-feasibility matrix:

  • High impact, high feasibility: Ready-to-eat meal delivery (addresses core problem directly, proven business model, clear operational path)
  • High impact, medium feasibility: Fitness integration feature (adds value but requires additional development and partnerships)
  • Medium impact, high feasibility: Meal kit delivery (easier to execute but doesn't solve time constraint)
  • Low impact, low feasibility: Complex AI planning systems (high development cost, uncertain user adoption)

Action Planning

After selecting the solution, we developed an action plan canvas to outline the implementation strategy. This plan identified key stakeholders, required resources, potential risks, success metrics, and the phased approach to bringing EasyMeals to market. The action plan helped us think through not just what to build, but how to build it systematically.

Final Solution Selection

We selected ready-to-eat meal delivery as the core solution because it directly addresses the time constraint while maintaining nutritional quality. The subscription model provides predictable revenue and user commitment. The "Gym Buddy" feature was included as a supporting feature to differentiate the offering and create a more holistic health solution.

Final Solution: EasyMeals

Solution Overview

EasyMeals is a subscription-based healthy meal delivery service that delivers ready-to-eat, nutritionally balanced meals to busy working professionals. The service removes the need for meal planning, grocery shopping, and cooking while ensuring consistent nutritional quality.

Subscription Business Model

  • Weekly plans: 5, 7, or 10 meals per week
  • Monthly plans: Discounted rates for longer commitments
  • Flexible pausing: Users can pause subscriptions for travel or schedule changes
  • Nutritional customization: Options for dietary restrictions and calorie targets

Key Features

Core Feature: Healthy Meal Delivery

  • Pre-prepared, nutritionally balanced meals
  • Delivered on a set schedule (weekly or bi-weekly)
  • Meals are ready to eat with minimal heating required
  • Nutritional information and ingredient transparency

Supporting Feature: "Gym Buddy"

  • In-app feature connecting fitness goals with meal recommendations
  • Meal suggestions aligned with workout schedules and fitness objectives
  • Progress tracking and motivation through integrated health metrics
  • Creates a more holistic health solution beyond just meal delivery

Storyboard: Visualizing the User Experience

To better understand and communicate the user experience, we created storyboards that visualize key moments in the user's journey with EasyMeals. These storyboards help illustrate the emotional journey, pain points, and moments of value delivery throughout the experience.

User Journey

Before EasyMeals

  • Sunday: Spend 2-3 hours meal planning and grocery shopping
  • Weekdays: 30-45 minutes daily for cooking and cleanup
  • Decision fatigue: Constant choices about what to eat
  • Inconsistent nutrition: Falling back to convenience foods when time is short

During EasyMeals Experience

  • Initial setup: One-time subscription selection and dietary preferences
  • Weekly delivery: Meals arrive on schedule, no decision-making required
  • Daily routine: Grab a meal from the fridge, heat if needed, eat within minutes
  • Gym Buddy integration: Meal recommendations align with workout schedule

After EasyMeals

  • Time savings: 5-7 hours per week reclaimed from meal planning and cooking
  • Consistent nutrition: Reliable access to balanced meals
  • Reduced stress: No daily decisions about meals
  • Better health outcomes: Improved energy levels and fitness progress

Feasibility & Viability

Technical and Operational Feasibility

  • Meal preparation: Central kitchen model with quality control processes (proven in existing meal delivery services)
  • Delivery logistics: Established delivery networks can be leveraged or built incrementally
  • Technology platform: Subscription management and app development are well-understood technical challenges
  • Scaling considerations: Kitchen capacity and delivery coverage need to scale with user base

Business Viability and Scalability

  • Market validation: Existing meal delivery services demonstrate market demand and willingness to pay
  • Revenue model: Subscription model provides predictable recurring revenue
  • Unit economics: Bulk preparation and delivery optimization can achieve sustainable margins
  • Competitive differentiation: Focus on nutritional quality and fitness integration differentiates from generic food delivery
  • Scalability path: Start with single-city operations, expand to multiple cities as operations mature

Key risks: Operational complexity (food safety, quality control), high customer acquisition costs, and need for consistent quality at scale. These are manageable with proper operational planning and gradual scaling.

Key Learnings

PM-Relevant Learnings

  • Problem framing matters: The HMW statement shaped the entire solution space. Taking time to reframe the problem correctly prevented us from building the wrong solution.
  • Constraints drive innovation: The time constraint forced us to eliminate solutions that seemed attractive but didn't address the core problem.
  • Business model is part of the product: The subscription model wasn't just a monetization strategy—it created user commitment and predictable behavior, which are product features.
  • Supporting features need clear rationale: "Gym Buddy" was included because it differentiated the offering, but it needed to be clearly secondary to the core value proposition.
  • Feasibility analysis prevents over-engineering: Evaluating solutions early prevented us from pursuing technically interesting but operationally complex ideas.

What I Would Improve Next

  • Quantitative validation: Conduct surveys to validate willingness to pay and price sensitivity before finalizing the business model
  • Operational deep-dive: Develop a more detailed operational plan including kitchen capacity, delivery logistics, and quality control processes
  • Competitive analysis: More thorough analysis of existing meal delivery services to identify specific differentiation opportunities
  • User testing: Create low-fidelity prototypes or service blueprints to test the user experience before full implementation
  • Financial modeling: Build a more detailed financial model including unit economics, customer acquisition costs, and break-even analysis
